Evolution of BMR


BMR stands for Basic Metabolic Rate, which means how much energy your body needs to maintain stability. The first BMR formula was developed in 1918-1919. It was called the "Harris-Benedict" equation, it takes the weight multiplied by 13.7516, height in centimeters and multiplied by 5.0033, age multiplied by 6.755, and you add 66.473 or 655.0955 depending on the gender.

The formula would look something like this:

    Men: (13.7516 x Weight) + (5.0033 * Height) - (6.755 * Age) + 66.473
    Women: (13.7516 x Weight) + (5.0033 * Height) - (6.755 * Age) + 655.0955

It was later developed in 1984 by Roza and Shizgal. It's known as the Revised Harris-Benedict Equation.

    Men: (13.397 * Weight) + (4.799 * Height) - (5.677 * Age) + 88.362
    Women: (9.247 * Weight) + (3.098 x Height) - (4.330 * Age) + 447.593

In 1990, the equation was once again revised but by Mifflin and St Jeor. This website uses this equation and it's called the Mifflin-St Jeor Equation. It's the most accurate out of the 3.
    Men: (10 * Weight) + (6.25 * Height) - (5 * Age) + 5
    Women: (10 * Weight) + (6.25 * Height) - (5 * Age) - 161

In brief, the equation appeared from a study by James Arthur Harris and Francis Gano Benedict, giving the first equation the name of "Harris-Benedict Equation." You can see the traces of the first equations spread across the 3, however a couple of things changed. The equations evolved in accuracy.
